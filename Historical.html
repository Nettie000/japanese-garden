HISTORY

Heian Period (794–1185): Japanese gardens were heavily influenced by Chinese landscape design. Large estates featured ponds, islands, and arrangements designed for poetic and seasonal appreciation. These were primarily gardens for the aristocracy.

Muromachi Period (1336–1573): With the rise of Zen Buddhism, dry landscape gardens gained prominence. The focus shifted toward abstraction, meditation, and monochrome aesthetics. The famous gardens at Ryōan-ji and Daitoku-ji are products of this era.

Momoyama & Edo Periods (1573–1867): Garden design became more elaborate and theatrical. The stroll garden (kaiyū-shiki) flourished, along with tea gardens influenced by wabi-cha aesthetics. Gardens became settings for artful living, blending nature, architecture, and ritual.

Modern Era (Meiji onward): After the Meiji Restoration, Japan opened to Western influences. Some gardens began to incorporate foreign elements, but many retained traditional structures and values. Today, Japanese gardens are found worldwide, from temple grounds and public parks to private homes and museums. They continue to serve as spaces for reflection and aesthetic appreciation.
